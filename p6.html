Phase 1

To start of, I scouted out locations around downtown San Jose that had special significance to me.

The Fallon House is across from San Pedro Square. It was built in the 1850s by one of San José’s earliest mayors, Thomas Fallon, and his wife Carmel Fallon. Thomas Fallon was a frontiersman in the John C. Fremont expedition. Carmel Fallon was the daughter of one of the most prominent Mexican landowners in California. The house showcases fifteen fully furnished rooms typical of the Victorian period.

Towne 3 Cinemas originally opened in 1926 to show silent movies.The cinema went through renovations through the years. IN 2009, Towne 3 started showing South Indian Bollywood movies. They also served Indian snacks and chai. However, due to due to Covid, the Towne 3 Cinemas have been permanently closed

The Circle of Palms marks the site where California’s first state legislature met in 1850. In 1849, during the states conception, San Jose was designated as California’s first capital. In the middle of the Circle of Palms is California’s seal.

Artist Statment

As California prepared for statehood in 1849, a conference was held in Monterey to draft the new state's constitution. San Jose was designated as the first state capital in the 1849 Constitution. The Circle of Palms is where the initial state capital of California was located from 1849 until 1851.

I decided to make a sign that embodied a banner with a coat of arms to explore a historical aesthetic. In the colors of Califonia’s flag, the coat of arms is divided into four quadrants. The first and fourth quadrants have the letters “S” and “J” in a font mimicking an old manuscript. The letters signify the abbreviation of San Jose. The third quadrant has a bear, which is reminiscent of California’s state animal the bear. The fourth quadrant has a star, similar to the stars that point to state capitals on a map. The star and the bear also share historical significance. The star, meaning sovereignty, and the bear, an abundant animal of California, share similarities to the Californian flag. The flag was raised in 1846 during the Bear Flag Revolt where a small group of American settlers rebelled against the Mexican government and declared California an independent republic. San Jose was the resulting capital of a newly independent California. Above the coat of arms, stands two palm trees, which is the marker for this meeting spot. Below the coat of arms is the date 1849, which is the year San Jose was made capital.

For the stamp, I had to simplify my design. Opting for the capital star as the centerpiece, I proceeded to place a circle of palms around the star. The star and palm trees work together to relay the message that the Circle of Palms is the marker for California’s first state capital.
